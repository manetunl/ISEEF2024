General informations

  • What is a stroke and what happens before it?

    It is a damage to a part of the brain, which occurs due to the interruption of blood flow to the brain for a long period, causing the wonderful function controlled by this part of the brain Transient ischemic attack (silent transient thrombotic stroke): occurs when blood flow to part of the brain stops for a short period of time, and temporary symptoms appear that last from a few minutes to a few hours, and do not cause permanent damage to the brain, as it is a serious warning sign that a stroke Stroke may occur in the future and should not be ignored. The cause is usually:
    Reduced blood flow in a narrow part of the main artery that carries blood to the brain. A blood clot travels from another part of the body (such as the heart) to the brain. Narrowing and blockage of small blood vessels in the brain; Which leads to difficulty blood flow for a short period of time,

  • You should seek emergency care immediately when you feel the following symptoms

    Numbness (paresthesia) or weakness in the face, arm, or leg, especially on one side of the body. 1) Difficulty speaking or understanding.
    2) Difficulty seeing in both or one eye.
    3) Difficulty walking, dizziness, and loss of balance.
    4)Sudden, severe headache that may be accompanied by nausea.

  • Types of stroke And Reason

    Ischemic (thrombotic) stroke: It occurs due to blockage of the blood vessels carrying blood to the brain, by fatty deposits or a blood clot (blood clot) originating from a place in the body, usually the heart. Hemorrhagic (bleeding) stroke: Bleeding is caused by a burst or ruptured blood vessel that results in swelling and pressure in the brain; Which leads to damage to cells and tissues, and in most cases may lead to death, and has several causes, one of which is irregular high blood pressure.

    the reason:
    The brain needs a continuous supply of oxygen and nutrients in order to function normally, and when a stroke occurs, the supply of oxygen to the brain stops, which leads to the death of brain cells after a few minutes. This results in a deficit in brain function, which may include problems with movement, speech, thinking, control of bowel and bladder functions, and other vital functions of the body, and the cause of its occurrence is still unknown.

  • Risk factors

    1- Advanced age, especially over 40 years.
    2- Family history of heart disease and stroke.
    3- Previous history of transient ischemic attacks (alarm stroke).
    4- Previous or current occurrence of blood clots.
    5- High blood pressure.
    6- Diabetes.
    7- High cholesterol.
    8- Cardiovascular disease (such as atrial fibrillation).
    9- Lack of physical activity.
    10- Obesity.
    11- Smoking.
    12- Taking drugs or drinking alcohol.

  • Symptoms and complications

    The signs and symptoms of a stroke often develop suddenly, and then may improve temporarily or worsen slowly, depending on the type of stroke and the affected area of ​​the brain. Knowing the signs and symptoms of a stroke can be life-saving, including:
    Sudden facial weakness or drooping, or vision problems.
    Sudden weakness or numbness in one or both arms.
    Difficulty speaking, slurred speech, or slurred speech.

    The infection is often in the chest and urinary tract.
    Swelling in the brain.
    Ulcers on the skin.
    Paralysis or loss of muscle movement.
    Difficulty speaking or swallowing.
    Memory loss and thinking difficulties.
    Depression.
    Clots in leg veins.

  • Evaluation and Treatment of Stroke

    The doctor needs to evaluate the type of stroke and the areas of the brain affected by it. He also needs to rule out other possible causes for the symptoms (such as: a brain tumor or a reaction to a drug). Therefore, the doctor may use several tests to determine the risk of stroke, including: CT scan, MRI, or cardiovascular tests.

    Treatment depends on the type of stroke and the time since symptoms began, but often includes: Medications: It is usually by taking one or more different medications, as some may be taken immediately and for a short period (such as: blood thinners), while others may need to be taken in the long term, but when diagnosed immediately, the affected person is given a clot buster. intravenous route; To improve blood flow to the part of the brain, but it should be used in the first three or four hours of the injury.
